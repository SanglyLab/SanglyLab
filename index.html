The hexagonal mosaic of the corneal endothelium

The Lab

A quantitative view of the ocular surface.

A single layer of hexagonal cells — the corneal endothelium — pumps fluid out of the cornea and holds a leaky barrier just tight enough to keep it transparent. When that balance breaks, the cornea swells and vision clouds. Our lab asks how the balance is held, how inflammation, oxidative stress, and hypothermia break it, and how it can be restored with drugs.

In parallel, we tackle a second problem that decides whether any eye medicine works at all: getting the drug to its target. We design mucoadhesive nanoparticle carriers for the ocular surface, model how molecules penetrate the cornea, and measure the barrier itself with custom fluorophotometry and impedance sensing.

Research Programs

Six questions at the heart of corneal & ocular science.

Our work spans the physiology of the anterior segment of the eye — from the molecular signaling that governs the endothelial barrier to the engineering of drug-delivery systems and the instruments that measure them.

Barrier Integrity of the Corneal Endothelium

Tight junctions and the perijunctional actomyosin ring (PAMR) restrain fluid leak into the stroma. We dissect how TNF-α, thrombin, and histamine remodel the actin cytoskeleton through Rho-GTPase, p38 MAP kinase, and myosin-light-chain signaling to break down the barrier — and how it can be pharmacologically rescued.

Fuchs Dystrophy & Oxidative Stress

Oxidative stress is a hallmark of Fuchs endothelial corneal dystrophy (FECD). Using cultured endothelia and donor tissue, we show how reactive oxygen species dismantle microtubules, the PAMR, and tight junctions — and test antioxidants and p38 MAPK inhibition as routes to protect endothelial function.

Aqueous Humor Outflow & the Trabecular Meshwork

Intraocular pressure is set by the resistance of the trabecular meshwork to aqueous humor outflow. We study how the contractile tone and signaling of trabecular meshwork cells regulate outflow — informing new strategies for lowering pressure in glaucoma.

Ocular Drug Delivery & Pharmacokinetics

Crossing the tear film and cornea is the central challenge of ophthalmic therapeutics. We engineer mucoadhesive silk-fibroin and PLGA nanoparticles for sustained delivery to the ocular surface, model corneal drug penetration, and probe the toxicity of preservatives such as benzalkonium chloride (BAK).

Ocular Fluorophotometry & Imaging

Building on a lineage of corneal fluorophotometry, we develop high-speed, multi-modal ocular fluorometers and quantitative imaging — including deep-learning segmentation of specular-microscopy images — to measure barrier function, endothelial morphology, and anterior-chamber inflammation in patients and animal models.

Corneal Preservation for Transplantation

Donor corneas deteriorate during cold storage before transplantation. We characterize how hypothermia and oxidative stress damage the endothelial cytoskeleton and barrier, and test microtubule-stabilizing agents and antioxidant additives to keep grafts healthier for longer.

Methods & Instrumentation

An engineer's toolkit for the eye.

A chemical-engineering foundation shapes how we measure biology — combining real-time biophysics, quantitative imaging, and materials fabrication under one roof.

  • Electric Cell–substrate Impedance Sensing (ECIS)

    Real-time transendothelial resistance to track barrier make-and-break as it happens.

  • Confocal Microfluorometry

    Custom fluorometers that resolve drug and dye penetration across the intact cornea.

  • Quantitative Immunocytochemistry & Live-Cell Imaging

    PAMR, ZO-1, and microtubule architecture; intracellular Ca²⁺ and pH dynamics.

  • Nanoparticle Engineering

    Silk-fibroin, PLGA, and metal nanoparticles for mucoadhesion and controlled release.

  • Machine Learning & Image Analysis

    Deep-learning segmentation of the endothelium and automated grading of barrier damage.

  • Primary Cell-Culture Models

    Bovine, porcine, and human corneal endothelial and epithelial monolayers.

Sangly P. Srinivas is a Professor in the School of Optometry at Indiana University Bloomington, where he leads a research program at the intersection of corneal cell physiology, ocular pharmacology, and drug delivery. Trained as a chemical engineer, he brings a quantitative, transport-and-signaling perspective to the biology of the ocular surface and anterior segment.

He earned his Ph.D. in Chemical Engineering from Drexel University (1987), then completed postdoctoral training at Stanford University — in ocular drug delivery, working with the pioneering corneal physiologist David M. Maurice — and at the University of California, Berkeley, in corneal physiology. That lineage runs directly into the lab's tools today, from corneal fluorophotometry to the biophysics of the endothelial barrier.

His research addresses (1) regulation of the barrier integrity of the corneal endothelium, (2) regulation of aqueous humor outflow by trabecular meshwork cells, and (3) the pharmacokinetics of topical drugs to the eye — with an emphasis on the pathophysiology and treatment of endothelial disorders, including Fuchs endothelial corneal dystrophy, and on high-speed, multi-modal ocular fluorometers for measurements in humans and animal models. As a member of the graduate faculty, he mentors doctoral students in the Vision Science program and collaborates with groups across the United States, India, Japan, and Thailand.
